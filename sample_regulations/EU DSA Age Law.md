**Core** **User** **Rights** **on** **Online** **Platforms**

> ● **Lodging** **Complaints:** Platforms must offer a clear and
> accessible contact point so you can easily reach them and complain or
> report any issue. This is outlined in Article 12.
>
> ● **Reporting** **Illegal** **Content:** If you encounter illegal
> content, such as counterfeit products or child sexual abuse materials,
> you can notify the platforms directly using their reporting
> mechanisms. This is covered under Article 16.
>
> ● **Understanding** **Decisions:** Platforms are required to explain
> their content moderation decisions clearly to all affected users. This
> is mandated by Article 17.

**Remedies** **and** **Representation**

> ● **Challenging** **Decisions:** If you disagree with a platform's
> content moderation decision, you can request a review using its
> internal complaint system. This right is found in Article 20.
>
> ● **Independent** **Dispute** **Settlement:** If the internal review
> does not lead to a satisfactory outcome, or if you simply choose to,
> you can turn to an independent dispute settlement body. This is
> detailed in Article 21.
>
> ● **Representation:** When exercising your rights, you may choose to
> be represented or assisted by a legal professional or an organization.
> This is supported by Article 86.

**Transparency** **and** **Choice**

> ● **Advertising** **Transparency:** Platforms must clearly indicate
> when content is advertising. They must also provide meaningful
> information about who paid for the ad and why you are seeing it. Very
> large platforms are additionally required to maintain public
> advertising libraries. These rules fall under Articles 26 & 39.
>
> ● **Content** **Recommendations:** Platforms must explain in clear
> terms the main parameters used in their content recommendation systems
> and how those parameters influence the content you are shown. Very
> large platforms must offer at least one recommendation option that is
> not based on user profiling. This is governed by Articles 27 & 38.

**Enforcement** **and** **Redress**

> ● **Complaining** **to** **Authorities:** If you suspect a breach of
> the DSA, you can submit a complaint to a national Digital Services
> Coordinator (DSC). This process is outlined in Article 53.
>
> ● **Going** **to** **Court:** You always retain the right to seek
> judicial redress under EU or national law. Courts remain the ultimate
> authority to review decisions taken by platforms and authorities.

**Reporting** **Systemic** **Issues**

> ● **Whistleblowers:** Insiders with knowledge of potential
> infringements can confidentially report information through the
> European Commission's secure whistleblower tool.
>
> ● **Incident** **Reporting:** Anyone can report systemic issues
> regarding potential infringements by designated very large online
> platforms (VLOPs) or very large online search engines (VLOSEs)
> directly to the European Commission via email.
>
> ● **CSO** **Reports:** Civil society organisations (CSOs) that conduct
> reports or studies related to the DSA can submit their findings
> through a designated CSO Online Form.
